Could a new typeface make it easier for the more than [400 million Arabic speakers](https://www.unesco.org/en/days/world-arabic-language) around the world to read?

Type designers [Dr. Nadine Chahine](https://arabictype.com/about/) and [Thomas Jockin](http://thomasjockin.com/) joined forces to find out. They created [Readex Pro](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Readex+Pro#about) in Arabic using the methodology behind [Lexend](https://design.google/library/lexend-readability/), made for Latin. The name Readex was chosen as a shortened form of “reading expanded.”

When Dr. Bonnie Shaver-Troup started the [Lexend project](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Lexend), her goal was to help people to read more easily and fluently by reducing visual noise. The Lexend fonts have distinct [letterforms](https://fonts.google.com/knowledge/glossary/letterform), and offer the option to widen [tracking](https://fonts.google.com/knowledge/glossary/tracking_letter_spacing) (the spacing between letters) together with widening the shapes of individual letterforms themselves. This novel functionality is based on a theory known as the “Shaver-Troup Formulation,” which was described in detail in a [2003 USA patent application](https://patents.google.com/patent/US20040253568A1/en?oq=10%2f462%2c865).

To learn more, read [The Design of Readex Pro](https://material.io/blog/readex-pro-legibility-arabic-type-design) (English) and [خط ‏Readex Pro: استكشاف حدود سهولة قراءة النص من خلال خط عربي جديد](https://blog.google/intl/ar-mena/company-news/outreach-initiatives/readex-arabic-accessibility/) (Arabic)
